Orientation

The big picture in one paragraph

A fiber internet business is a ladder of technology: the OLT hardware that lights up neighborhoods, the ONT and router in the subscriber’s home, the management software (an EMS) that configures those devices, and cloud software that turns the torrent of data those devices emit into something an internet provider can act on. An operations cloud is the piece built for the network operations team: it watches network health, raises and correlates alarms, predicts problems with ML, and drives operations like adding a subscriber or activating a service. My job is testing its backend: the APIs, pipelines, and services between the network and the dashboard.

🖍️ Like you’re five Imagine a huge toy train set (the internet cables) delivering candy (internet) to every house in town. Someone builds the train station, the tracks, and the mailbox at each house — and a magic control room in the sky that watches every train at once. My job? I’m the kid who tries to break the control room on purpose, before real customers ever see it break.
Lesson 0 · Basic electronics & optics

The Light Lab: why glass beat copper

🖍️ Like you’re five You and your friend have flashlights and a secret code: blink = 1, dark = 0. Now imagine a flashlight that can blink ten billion times every second, shining down a noodle of glass so pure you could see through 20 kilometers of it. That’s fiber internet. That’s the whole trick.

🔦 Send your name as light

Every letter is secretly a number, and every number is secretly blinks. Type a letter (or your initial) and fire the laser:

A = 01000001
🔦 👁️

Bright dot = laser ON = 1 · hollow dot = laser OFF = 0. A real PON laser does this ~10,000,000,000× per second. The 👁️ is a photodiode — an electric eye that turns light back into bits.

The grown-up version: attenuation, wavelengths, dBm

Attenuation — signal fading with distance — is fiber’s superpower. Copper DSL degrades badly after a few hundred meters; fiber loses so little light (~0.35 dB/km) that one OLT port serves homes 20 km away with nothing powered in between. Glass is also immune to electromagnetic interference (lightning and motors can’t induce noise into it), and can carry many signals at once on different wavelengths (colors) — which is exactly how upload and download share one strand without colliding.

Field vocabulary: light levels are measured in dBm (received power: −18 dBm healthy, −28 dBm trouble); the optical budget is how much loss a link can tolerate; a splice or a dirty connector “eats budget.” When a tech says an ONT has low light, the operations cloud is often what told them.

🌀 The Bend Lab: why light stays in the noodle

Light stays inside glass by total internal reflection — hitting the wall at a shallow angle, it bounces back in, forever. Bend the fiber too hard, though, and the bounce angle gets too steep: photons leak out. Field techs call this macrobend loss — the classic “someone stapled the patch cord” ticket. GPON vs XGS-PON (the generations)

GPON (Gigabit PON) is the workhorse generation: 2.5 Gbps down / 1.25 Gbps up, shared across the splitter. XGS-PON is the current mainstream upgrade: 10 Gbps symmetric (X = 10 in Roman numerals, G = gigabit, S = symmetric). They use different light colors, so both can run on the same glass at the same time — that’s how providers upgrade neighborhoods without digging up streets. Vendors market this flexibility as “any-PON.” Backend relevance: every device record carries its PON type, and provisioning logic and bandwidth profiles differ by generation — a classic source of test permutations.

🌈 Colors on one glass

How do downloads, uploads, GPON and XGS-PON all share one strand without colliding? Different wavelengths — literally different colors of (invisible) light. Toggle them:

All four can flow simultaneously, forever, and never interfere — like four radio stations on different frequencies. This is why an operator can overlay XGS-PON on a GPON neighborhood without touching the glass.

Field tool · real math techs use

🔦 The Optical Budget Calculator

🖍️ Like you’re five Your flashlight starts bright, but every kilometer of noodle, every splitter, and every smudgy connector steals a little light. If too much gets stolen before it reaches the house, the mailbox can’t read the blinks. This calculator is the “will there be enough light left?” game — and it’s the actual arithmetic behind every fiber build plan and half of all “low light” trouble tickets.

Received power at the ONT = transmit power − everything the path eats. Drag the sliders and watch the light meter. Healthy ONTs want roughly −8 to −24 dBm; below ~−28 dBm the link dies.

0−8−16−24−28−35 dBm

Assumptions: OLT launches at +3 dBm · glass eats 0.35 dB/km · each splitter doubling eats ~3.5 dB (1:32 ≈ 17.5 dB) · splice 0.1 dB · clean connector 0.5 dB. Simplified but true to life — real planners add a safety margin on top. This is what “that splice ate our optical budget” means.

Lesson 2 · Basic networking

The vocabulary the packets travel on

🖍️ Like you’re five Think of the network as a giant apartment building. VLANs are colored hallways — kids in the blue hallway can’t wander into the red one. DHCP is the front desk handing you a name tag (“you are 192.168.1.7 today!”). And provisioning is the building manager setting up your apartment before you move in: turning on the lights, labeling your mailbox, choosing how fast your elevator goes.
Layers, in 60 seconds

Networking is described in layers. Layer 1 is the physical signal — our laser light. Layer 2 (Ethernet) moves frames between directly connected devices using MAC addresses — a PON is essentially a giant shared Layer-2 segment. Layer 3 (IP) routes packets across networks using IP addresses. When an ONT “comes up,” that’s Layer 1/2; when the subscriber’s router gets an IP address, that’s Layer 3. Operations-cloud health data spans all of them: light levels (L1), frame errors (L2), unreachable devices (L3).

VLANs, DHCP, and what “provisioning” actually means

A VLAN is a virtual fence inside a shared network: traffic tagged VLAN 100 can’t see traffic tagged VLAN 200 even on the same wire. Providers use VLANs to separate services (internet vs video vs voice). DHCP is how a device asks “may I have an IP address?” — when a new subscriber’s router can’t get one, that’s a top-tier support call.

Provisioning is the industry word for “pushing the configuration that turns a service on”: which PON port, which VLAN, what speed (bandwidth profile), what quality guarantees (QoS). When the operations cloud “adds a service,” what physically happens is provisioning — config flowing down to the OLT and ONT.

Management protocols: how software talks to boxes

Three protocol families matter here. NETCONF/YANG is the modern, structured way to configure network equipment — YANG defines the schema (“an interface has a name, a VLAN, a speed…”), NETCONF is the transaction protocol that reads/writes it. The EMS speaks NETCONF southbound to the OLTs. TR-069 (and its successor TR-369/USP) is how an ACS manages in-home devices — Wi-Fi settings, firmware, reboots — at millions-of-devices scale. SNMP is the legacy monitoring protocol older devices still use. A backend tester meets all three as southbound dependencies to mock or simulate.

The industry ladder

The broadband stack, bottom to top

Every fiber operator on Earth assembles this same four-tier ladder. Vendors differ, product names differ — the shape never does. Learn the shape once and you can read any operator’s architecture diagram.

The QA lens · applies at any broadband software shop

What “testing the backend” means here

🖍️ Like you’re five I don’t test by clicking buttons and hoping. I test like a mischievous gremlin with a clipboard: I unplug things mid-sentence, send the same letter twice, mail gibberish, and shout 4,000 alarms at once — then check the cloud stayed calm, told the truth, and never left a family half-connected.

The recurring theme: this backend’s hardest problems are distributed-systems problems — eventual consistency between cloud state and device truth, retries and idempotency, correctness under partial failure. Good tests here are less “click and check,” more “inject, wait, reconcile, assert.”
